Baking Soda

Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is a powerful leavening agent. When combined with an acid, it produces carbon dioxide bubbles, helping your baked goods rise beautifully.

Tip: For best results, use baking soda with acidic ingredients like buttermilk, yogurt, lemon juice, or vinegar.

Function: Leavening | Substitutes: Baking powder + vinegar | Common in: Cookies, quick breads, and egg-based recipes like pancakes

Eggs

Eggs are a staple in baking, providing structure, richness, moisture, and color. The proteins in eggs help bind ingredients together, while the yolks add fat and flavor.

Tip: Room temperature eggs mix better into batter, leading to more consistent textures.

Function: Binding, Moisture, Leavening | Substitutes: Applesauce, mashed banana, flax eggs | Common in: Cakes, muffins, custards

Butter

Butter adds richness, flavor, and tenderness to baked goods. Its fat content helps with texture and contributes to leavening when creamed with sugar.

Tip: For flaky pastries, keep butter cold before mixing into dough.

Function: Fat, Flavor | Types: Salted, Unsalted | Common in: Cookies, cakes, pie crusts

Oil

Oil keeps baked goods moist and tender. Unlike butter, it remains liquid at room temperature, which affects the final texture of your bake.

Tip: Use neutral oils like canola or vegetable oil for cakes and quick breads.

Function: Moisture, Fat | Types: Vegetable, Canola, Olive | Common in: Brownies, muffins, cakes
